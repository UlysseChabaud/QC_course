Quantum Computing I - Lecture notes

0 Introduction

Quantum computing is a rapidly developing field that has generated a lot of hype in recent years. It may hold the potential to revolutionise various industries, from finance and healthcare to communications and cybersecurity. However, despite the excitement surrounding quantum computing, it is still in its early stages, and there is much we do not know about the technology and its potential uses.

In this context, it is essential to take a step back and ask some fundamental questions:

What is a quantum computation? Why build quantum computers? How are quantum computers going to be useful?

In this course, we will explore these questions and provide an overview of the field of quantum computing.

1 What is a quantum computation?

There is not a single answer to that question. The standard ways of modelling quantum computations with qubits include the quantum circuit model, measurement-based quantum computing and adiabatic quantum computing. Note that there are many others, and that qubits are far from being the only option for manipulating quantum information.

1.1 Qubit quantum computation

In a classical computer, the most basic unit of information is a bit, which can take two values: 000﻿ and 111﻿. In a quantum computer, the laws of quantum physics allow phenomena like superposition and entanglement. When discussing information processing in a quantum world, the most basic unit is therefore a quantum bit, usually called qubit, a two-level quantum system with a ground state ∣0⟩|0\rangle∣0⟩﻿ and an excited state ∣1⟩|1\rangle∣1⟩﻿. Unlike a classical bit, which only has two possible states, a quantum bit has infinitely many states: all superpositions of ∣0⟩|0\rangle∣0⟩﻿ and ∣1⟩|1\rangle∣1⟩﻿,

There are many physical implementations of qubits, e.g., superconducting qubits, trapped ions, natural atoms, photons, etc. For now, we assume that we have access to qubits without caring too much about how they are made.

1.1.1 The quantum circuit model

Introduction

Loosely speaking, a computation requires a system that can represent data, a way to perform manipulation of that data, and a method for reading out the result of the computation. In the circuit model of quantum computation we use:

  • Qubits to represent the data.
  • State preparation to initialize the qubits in the input state we need to begin the computation.
  • Quantum gates on the qubits to manipulate the data.
  • Measurements on the qubits to read out the final result.

For brevity, we assume that it is possible to initialize our quantum computer in some simple state, e.g., ∣000...00⟩|000...00\rangle∣000...00⟩﻿, and that we can read out the state of the qubits at the end of a computation in the computational basis ∣0⟩,∣1⟩|0\rangle,|1\rangle∣0⟩,∣1⟩﻿.

Measurements

In the quantum circuit model, the measurement is typically assumed to be in the computational basis. It assigns the observed quantum state to a single value, according to the Born rule: a measurement of a quantum state ∣ψ⟩=α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩|\psi\rangle=\alpha|0\rangle+\beta|1\rangle∣ψ⟩=α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩﻿ in the computational basis gives the outcome 000﻿ with probability ∣α∣2|\alpha|^2∣α∣2﻿ and the outcome 111﻿ with probability ∣β∣2|\beta|^2∣β∣2﻿.

The application of a quantum gate can be conditioned on the classical outcome of a measurement.

Universal quantum circuits

Are all the gates we specified above enough to carry out any quantum computation that we would like? Could we do any quantum computation using just a small subset of the gates above? These are questions about universality.

For classical computers, a set of gates is called universal if, by applying enough gates from this set in a sequence, it is possible to express any Boolean function on any number of bits. The classical TOFFOLI gate turns out to be universal all on its own, but there are other sets of gates that are not universal. For example, the set {AND,OR} is not universal, because these gates cannot express non-monotone Boolean functions; changing an input bit from 0 to 1 in a circuit with these gates will never result in an output bit changing from 1 to 0. For quantum computers, a gate set is called universal if the gates therein can be used to approximate any unitary transformation on any number of qubits up to any desired precision. To understand what makes a gate set universal for quantum computing, let us first see how a gate set can fail to be universal.

  • Inability to create superposition states: some gates, e.g., XXX﻿ and CNOT\text{CNOT}CNOT﻿, map all states in the computational basis into other states in the computational basis (e.g, CNOT∣11⟩=∣10⟩\text{CNOT}|11\rangle=|10\rangleCNOT∣11⟩=∣10⟩﻿). These gates can maintain superpositions, but they cannot create new ones.

1.1.2 Measurement-based quantum computing

Introduction

In the previous section, we have encountered the circuit model for performing quantum computations. From the point of view of computer science this is the most natural model, as it is a direct analogue of the classical circuit model on which the architecture of our (classical) computers is based. However, quantum information allows for other, much more exotic, models of computation. Many of these models were originally proposed with the idea that they might be more powerful than the circuit model, although ultimately they were proved equivalent. This includes adiabatic quantum computing which we cover in the next section, and measurement-based quantum computing (MBQC) that we will now study.

In the circuit model of quantum computation, we prepare an initial state, apply a sequence of quantum gates to this state, and finally do some measurement on the output state to obtain the result of the computation. While this seems a natural order of operations, which agrees with our intuition for how a classical computation is performed, it is possible to mix up the order somewhat.

In MBQC, we first prepare a certain fixed entangled state of qubits, known as a graph state, where a subset of these qubits represent the input state. This entangled state is the resource for our computation. In the rest of the computation, we never need to apply any multi-qubit operations. All we do is apply single-qubit rotations and single-qubit measurements in some sequence, where later rotations are conditioned on earlier measurement results. The output state will be encoded in a subset of the qubits (different from the subset that encoded the input), and a final measurement can be performed on this output state to read out the result of the computation.

Because of the presence of measurements in earlier steps of the computation, MBQC differs from the circuit model in one important aspect: it is not reversible. In the circuit model, we apply a large unitary transformation (decomposed into a sequence of gates) that takes us from the input state to the output state. Before any measurement is done on the output state, this unitary transformation could be reversed to bring us back to the input state. However, since measurements are done before we reach the output state in MBQC, that computation cannot be reversed. For this reason, MBQC has been introduced as one-way quantum computation [Raussendorf2001]. More details can be found in the follow-up paper [Raussendorf2003]. The first experimental demonstration of all basic components of MBQC was performed in 2005 [Walther2005].

Let’s give an overview of how this model works.

Multi-qubit MBQC

What if we have a multi-qubit computation? Recall that a single entangling gate is enough to provide universality together with all single-qubit operations. Since the CZ\text{CZ}CZ﻿ gate comes for free in MBQC, we can implement any computation by combining the technique from the previous section with additional gates. However, in that case, the underlying graph state will be highly dependent on the computation to be performed. As it turns out, there exist simple graph states that are universal resources for MBQC, meaning that one can embed any quantum computation within the same graph structure. Then, it is sufficient to tune the measurement angles

One such graph state is the brickwork state: we use multiple lines of qubits, one per qubit of the computation; the lines are connected by vertical CZ\text{CZ}CZ﻿ operations once every three qubits in a slightly shifted manner.

This graph allows to implement single-qubit unitary operations as before, by setting the first three angles appropriately and all remaining angles to 000﻿. Moreover, a two-qubit CNOT\text{CNOT}CNOT﻿ gate can be applied using similar ideas as we described, but performing measurements on the two lines associated with both qubits on which the gate acts. We leave the details as an exercise below. Finally, the concatenation of brickwork states allows to implement gates in sequence.

The brickwork state is not the only universal resource state for MBQC. For instance, it has been shown that a square lattice graph state (a cluster state) is a universal resource for quantum computation. Despite this fact, depending on the specific kind of computation, other graphs than a square lattice could be more suitable for implementing the computation. Note that not all graph states are equally useful for quantum computing, and there are powerful tools to determine whether a given graph state can give rise to deterministic MBQC [Browne2007].

It is clear that MBQC requires much more qubits than the circuit model to perform the same computation. However, these qubits can be measured earlier, necessitating lower coherence time than in the circuit picture. Moreover, it can be shown (for instance through the brickwork state construction) that any quantum circuit can be implemented in MBQC with at most a polynomial overhead in terms of number of qubits. Reciprocally, through the deferred measurement principle, any MBQC can be turned into a quantum circuit efficiently, by pushing all the measurements to the end of the computation. While fundamentally different in nature, those two models of quantum computations thus are equivalent from a computational point of view!
