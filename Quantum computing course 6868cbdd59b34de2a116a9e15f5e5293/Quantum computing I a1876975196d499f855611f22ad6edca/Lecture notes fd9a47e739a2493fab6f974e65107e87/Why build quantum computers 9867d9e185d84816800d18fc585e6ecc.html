Why build quantum computers?

We have seen that here are many ways to represent a quantum computation. But why build quantum computers in the first place?

The answer is: quantum computational advantage, that is the ability of quantum computers to outperform their classical counterparts. How do we know this is possible? There are famous quantum algorithms, such as Shor’s algorithm or Grover’s algorithm, which can solve some computational problems faster than the best known classical algorithms.

However these algorithms typically require the use of a universal quantum computer, which will not be available in the near future, so their existence does not demonstrate that near-term quantum computers are going to be outperforming classical ones any time soon. It could even be the case that quantum mechanics breaks down for large systems, such that a universal quantum computer cannot be built. We thus need a more convincing argument, which as we will see in this section is provided by sub-universal quantum models.

2.1 Sub-universal models

Sub-universal quantum models are rudimentary, special-purpose quantum computers, simpler to build than their universal counterparts, but still powerful enough to perform specific computations that are intractable for our classical computers. These machines should also be programmable stepping stones towards universal quantum computers, otherwise one could argue that a teapot could give us computational advantage in a similar fashion.

There are many examples of such sub-universal models in the literature, including:

  • Instantaneous quantum polytime (IQP) circuits [Shepherd2008]
  • Boson Sampling [Aaronson2010]
  • Random circuit sampling [Boixo2016]

These models have given rise to various claims of quantum speedup demonstration [Arute2019,Zhong2020Wu2021Zhu2021Madsen2022] which are still being debated due to imperfect benchmarks of the corresponding experiments and progress of classical simulation algorithms.

In the following sections, we focus on the Boson Sampling model.

2.2.4 Complexity-theoretic foundations of quantum computational advantage

In this section, we provide a compelling argument that Boson Sampling computers can dramatically outperform classical ones based on classical computational complexity theory. Computational complexity theory focuses on classifying computational problems according to their resource usage, and relating these complexity classes to each other.

Complexity classes

Informally, a complexity class is a set of computational problems which share common features. Given a complexity class C\textsf CC﻿, we say that a problem PPP﻿ is C\textsf CC﻿-hard if any problem in C\textsf CC﻿ can be reduced in polynomial time to that problem PPP﻿. If additionally P∈CP\in\textsf CP∈C﻿, we say that PPP﻿ is C\textsf CC﻿-complete. Finally, a C\textsf CC﻿ machine is an abstract machine that can solve any problem in C\textsf CC﻿ in polynomial time.

  • P\textsf PP﻿ is the class of decision problems, i.e., with binary answers, that can be solved in polynomial time by a classical computer (formally, a Turing machine, which is the standard abstract model of a classical computer). Informally, this is the class of problems that can be solved efficiently by a classical computer (equivalently, classical computers are P\textsf PP﻿ machines). P\textsf PP﻿ includes linear optimisation problems, determining whether an integer is prime, computing the determinant, etc.
  • NP\textsf{NP}NP﻿ is the class of problems with binary answers, such that a solution can be verified in polynomial time by a classical computer. Informally, this is the class of problems whose solutions can be verified efficiently by a classical computer. The following problem, which is the decision version of the Max-Cut problem we encountered in Section 1.1.3., is NP\textsf{NP}NP﻿-complete: “Given a graph GGG﻿ and an integer kkk﻿, determine whether there is a cut of size at least kkk﻿ in GGG﻿.” NP\textsf{NP}NP﻿ additionally includes all problems in P\textsf PP﻿, but also well-known problems such as sudoku solving, the travelling salesman problem, integer factorization, etc.

P\textsf PP﻿ versus NP\textsf{NP}NP﻿ is a major unsolved problem in theoretical computer science. In informal terms, it asks whether every problem whose solution can be quickly verified can also be quickly solved. It is widely believed that this is not the case, i.e., P≠NP\textsf P\neq\textsf{NP}P=NP﻿:

  • PH\textsf{PH}PH﻿ is the so-called polynomial hierarchy of complexity classes. It is an infinite hierarchy PH=⋃k=0+∞PHk\textsf{PH}=\bigcup_{k=0}^{+\infty}\textsf{PH}_kPH=⋃k=0+∞​PHk​﻿ of complexity classes with PH0⊂PH1⊂PH1⊂…\textsf{PH}_0\subset\textsf{PH}_1\subset\textsf{PH}_1\subset\dotsPH0​⊂PH1​⊂PH1​⊂…﻿.
