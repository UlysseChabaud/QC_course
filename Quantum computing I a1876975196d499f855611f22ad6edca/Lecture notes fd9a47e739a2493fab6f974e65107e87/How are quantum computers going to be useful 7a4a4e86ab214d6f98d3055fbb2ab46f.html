How are quantum computers going to be useful?

We don’t really know the full potential and limitations of quantum computers yet. But we can try a few things: beyond the well-known algorithms for factoring (Shor) and unstructured search (Grover), there are many areas that could potentially benefit from the advent of quantum computers. The anticipated applications of quantum computers span across science and industry, ranging from quantum chemistry and many-body physics to optimization, finance, and machine learning.

In what follows, we present a few particular examples of applications of quantum computers. We refer the reader to [Dalzell2023] for a detailed survey of current quantum applications and algorithms.

3.1 Quantum applications

In the near-term, quantum computers are limited to being Noisy, Intermediate-Scale Quantum devices. This is the so-called NISQ era. In that context, quantum algorithms are typically heuristic.

3.2 Quantum primitives

Quantum algorithms can be thought of as a combination of quantum primitives, including:

  • Block encoding: encodes a linear operator in a unitary operator.
  • Quantum Fourier transform: performs the Fourier transform on kets.
  • Quantum phase estimation: produces an estimate of an eigenvalue of a unitary operator.
  • Amplitude amplification: boosts or extracts the amplitude of a marked quantum state that is produced in superposition with orthogonal state(s) by a unitary matrix.
  • Quantum adiabatic evolution: (approximately) prepares an eigenstate (typically the ground state) of a particular Hamiltonian.
  • Quantum tomography: finds a full classical description of a quantum state (or quantum channel) by extracting information by means of repeated measurements.
  • Variational quantum circuits: optimizes a parametrized quantum circuit by repeated runs of the circuit.
  • Quantum random access memory: enables coherent access to classical data, such that multiple different elements in a classical database can be read in superposition.
  • Quantum singular value transformation: applies a polynomial transformation to the singular values of a linear operator embedded in a larger unitary operator.

In what follows we focus on the quantum singular value transformation (QSVT), which encompasses many other quantum primitives. Its introduction has been referred to as “a grand unification of quantum algorithms” [Martyn2021].

As written above, the QSVT algorithm efficiently applies a polynomial transformation to the singular values of a linear operator (governing a particular subsystem) embedded in a larger unitary operator. The polynomial transformation of the singular values is achieved by applying a specific sequence of SU(2)SU(2)SU(2)﻿ rotations to the embedded subspace, where each rotation is parameterized by an angle ϕk∈R\phi_k ∈\mathbb Rϕk​∈R﻿.

The QSVT algorithm is parametric in that the polynomial transformation is completely characterized by the choice of phase angles ϕk{\phi_k}ϕk​﻿. Moreover, given the desired polynomial transformation, the QSP phase angles which generate it may be classically efficiently and stably computed.

This parametrisation provides immense flexibility to the QSVT: using QSVT as a subroutine, quantum algorithms for the search problem, phase estimation, Hamiltonian simulation and matrix inversion can be derived.
